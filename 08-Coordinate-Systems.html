Coordinate Systems

Slides Outline

  • In the previous lecture and the current tutorial, we are looking at flow problems in rectangular coordinates:
    • Pressure driven flow between two plates with/without a moving top plate.
    • Gravity driven flow of a film over a plate.
  • In the film flow problem we found that locating the coordinates in the correct location can greatly simplify the problem.
  • Here, there was a translational symmetry in the system, and rotating two axis of the system into this plane simplified the problem (the x and z coordinates could be ignored).
  • This is a key concept. When we try to look at a new system we should identify the symmetry and locate and orientate our axis to exploit this!
  • But what about non-planar symmetry? For example, take fluid flow in a pipe:
  • Here we have an axis of symmetry .
  • Even if we locate our rectangular coordinate system on the axis of symmetry and rotate one axis to align down the symmetry axis the problem will not get simpler.
  • For example, given the Cartesian coordinate system highlighted above, the boundary of the pipe must be described using the equation of a circle!
  • The no-slip condition has to be stated like so: \begin{align*} \boldsymbol{v}&=0 & \text{when $\sqrt{x^2 + y^2} = R$} \end{align*}
  • This boundary condition is difficult to work with, its much simpler to transform the system into a more convenient coordinate system.
  • The symmetry boundary condition is not easy to express either: \begin{align*} \tau_{ij}&=0 & \text{when $i,j\in[x,y] $ AND $x=0$ AND $y=0$} \end{align*}
  • This boundary condition arises as the system is symmetric about and axis (there is no transport across points/lines/planes of symmetry).
  • We have to specify quite a few conditions on this condition to make it correct.
  • Lets try a more natural coordinate system…
  • It should be obvious that the most convenient coordinates to work in in this case are cylindrical coordinates .
  • And this is a general rule, if we have
    • Translational symmetry in a plane we should use rectangular coordinates .
    • Rotational symmetry about an axis we should use cylindrical coordinates .
    • Rotational symmetry about a point we should use spherical coordinates .
  • But how do we use cylindrical coordinates in our balance equations?
  • To learn how to use them, we must derive the general balance equation in cylindrical coordinate systems, and then compare the results to see if we can find some pattern.
  • It actually turns out that we can use our existing balance equations, but we first must prove this!
  • Just as before, we will look at creating a general balance of a property $B$ over a cylindrical control volume .
  • Lets start by defining the cylindrical coordinate system properly…
  • In rectangular coordinates we can specify a point using ($x$ , $y$ , $z$ ) coordinates (this axis is highlighted on the diagram).
  • Note that the directions $\hat{\boldsymbol{x}}$ , $\hat{\boldsymbol{y}}$ , and $\hat{\boldsymbol{z}}$ are always the same.
  • In cylindrical coordinates, we use a radius $r$ , angle $\theta$ and axial offset $z$ .
  • Please note that the directions $\hat{\boldsymbol{r}}$ and $\hat{\boldsymbol{\theta}}$ change with position, but $\hat{\boldsymbol{z}}$ is still easy.
  • Therefore, to generate a balance equation we will need to consider the fluxes over a small volume from ($r$ , $\theta$ , $z$ ) to
    *( $r+\delta r$ , $\theta+\delta\theta$ , $z+\delta z$ ).
Note We have already assumed that $\Delta r\to 0$ , $\Delta \theta\to 0$ , and $\Delta z\to 0$ , hence why we are not using $\Delta r$ but $\delta r$ instead. This is the “correct” way to do these derivations.
  • This is the control volume for cylindrical coordinates.
  • The main difference when compared to the rectangular control volume is what happens in the $r$ -direction.
  • Here, the volume is in the shape of an arc !
  • The length of an arc is the radius times by the angle.
  • So we have an inner arc of $r\delta\theta$ and an outer arc of $(r+\delta r)\delta\theta$ !
  • In a moment we will look at the fluxes through these faces, so we need to be careful about their areas…
  • The $rz$ faces (highlighted in green) are the simplest faces to deal with as they are square!
  • The area of each of these faces is simply \begin{align*} A_{rz}=\delta{}r \delta{}z \end{align*}
  • The $\theta z$ faces are a curved, and so have an area equal to the arc length times the height.
  • This means the inner and outer areas differ! \begin{align*} A_{\theta z}(r)&=r \delta\theta \delta z\\ A_{\theta z}(r+\delta r)&=(r+\delta r)\delta\theta \delta z \end{align*}
  • The $r\theta$ face areas are equal to the difference of two sectors .
  • The expression for the area is \begin{align*} A_{r\theta} &= \frac{1}{2}\left((r+\delta r)^2 - r^2\right)\delta\theta = \frac{1}{2}\left(2 r \delta r + \delta r^2\right)\delta\theta \end{align*}
  • However, we've already assumed that $\delta r\to 0$ precisely for this part of the derivation.
  • The rule is that any differential difference terms which are second order are so small they will go to zero!
  • This means that the area of this face is \begin{align*} A_{r\theta} &= = \frac{1}{2}\left(2 r \delta r + \cancelto{0}{\delta r^2}\right)\delta\theta = r \delta r \delta\theta \end{align*}
  • We've solved for the areas of each face, but what about the control volume?
  • The control volume is simply the $A_{r\theta}$ times by the height $\delta z$ ! \begin{align*} \delta V &= A_{r\theta} \delta z\\% &= r \delta r \delta\theta \delta z \end{align*}
  • We now have all of the areas and volumes we need to consider for the cylindrical balance!
  • Before we can perform our balances, we must note that we have already taken the limit that our differential elements go to zero.
  • This means we can no longer use the definition of the first derivative \begin{align*} \frac{\partial f}{\partial x} &= \lim_{\Delta x\to 0}\frac{f(x+\Delta x)-f(x)}{\Delta x} \end{align*} as we cannot take the limit twice!
  • However, lets take look at the Taylor expansion of a flux \begin{align*} J_{B,x}(x+\Delta x) &= J_{B,x}(x) + \Delta x \frac{\partial J_{B,x}(x)}{\partial x}+\frac{\Delta x^2}{2} \frac{\partial^2 J_{B,x}(x)}{\partial x^2}+… \end{align*}
  • As we take the limit $\Delta x\to 0$ , we find that \begin{align*} J_{B,x}(x+\delta x) &= J_{B,x}(x) + \delta x \frac{\partial J_{B,x}(x)}{\partial x} \end{align*} As before, all of the $\delta x^2$ terms (and higher) will go to zero as they are extremely small.
  • We now have a relationship to give us the definition of a gradient in terms of differential changes: \begin{align*} \frac{J_{B,x}(x+\delta x) - J_{B,x}(x)}{\delta x} &= \frac{\partial J_{B,x}(x)}{\partial x} \end{align*}
  • Lets start with $J_{B,\theta}$ , we have from our flux in and out \begin{align*} \text{IN}_\theta-\text{OUT}_\theta &= A_{rz}\left[J_{B,\theta}(\theta)-J_{B,\theta}(\theta+\delta\theta)\right] \end{align*} and we have from the Taylor expansion \begin{align*} J_{B,\theta}(\theta+\delta \theta) &= J_{B,\theta}(\theta) + \delta \theta \frac{\partial J_{B,\theta}(\theta)}{\partial \theta} \end{align*}
  • Substituting the expansion in and cancelling we have \begin{align*} \text{IN}_\theta-\text{OUT}_\theta &= - A_{rz} \delta\theta \frac{\partial J_{B,\theta}(\theta)}{\partial \theta}\\ &= -\delta{}r \delta{}z \delta\theta \frac{\partial J_{B,\theta}(\theta)}{\partial \theta} \end{align*}
  • Next is the $z$ direction, we have \begin{align*} \text{IN}_z-\text{OUT}_z &= A_{r\theta}\left[J_{B,z}(z)-J_{B,z}(z+\delta z)\right] \end{align*} and we have from the Taylor expansion \begin{align*} J_{B,z}(z+\delta z) &= J_{B,z}(z) + \delta z \frac{\partial J_{B,z}(z)}{\partial z} \end{align*}
  • Substituting the expansion in and cancelling we have \begin{align*} \text{IN}_z-\text{OUT}_z &= - A_{r\theta} \delta z \frac{\partial J_{B,z}(z)}{\partial z}\\ &= -r \delta r \delta\theta \delta z \frac{\partial J_{B,z}(z)}{\partial z} \end{align*}
  • Finally it is the $r$ direction's turn, here we have a special case as the flux areas are different \begin{align*} \text{IN}_r-\text{OUT}_r &= A_{\theta z}(r) J_{B,r}(r) -A_{\theta z}(r+\delta r) J_{B,r}(r+\delta r) \end{align*} where $A_{\theta z}(r) = r \delta\theta \delta z$ and we have from the Taylor expansion \begin{align*} J_{B,r}(r+\delta r) &= J_{B,r}(r) + \delta r \frac{\partial J_{B,r}(r)}{\partial r} \end{align*}
  • Substituting the expansion and area functions \begin{align*} \text{IN}_r-\text{OUT}_r &= r \delta\theta \delta z  J_{B,r}(r) -(r+\delta r) \delta\theta \delta z \left(J_{B,r}(r) + \delta r \frac{\partial J_{B,r}(r)}{\partial r}\right) \end{align*}
  • Expanding and removing any terms where there is a $\delta r^2$ term gives \begin{align*} \text{IN}_r-\text{OUT}_r &= -\delta r \delta\theta \delta z\left(J_{B,r}(r) + r  \frac{\partial J_{B,r}(r)}{\partial r}\right) \end{align*}
  • We can use the product rule identity that $f(x)+x f'(x) = (x f(x))'$ to write the final result \begin{align*} \text{IN}_r-\text{OUT}_r &= -\delta\theta \delta z \delta r \frac{\partial  r J_{B,r}(r)}{\partial r} \end{align*}
  • Nearly done, adding that all together we have \begin{align*} \text{ACCUMULATION} &= \text{INPUT}_r - \text{OUTPUT}_r \\% &+ \text{INPUT}_\theta - \text{OUTPUT}_\theta\\% &+ \text{INPUT}_z - \text{OUTPUT}_z \\% &+ \text{GENERATION} \end{align*}
  • Substituting in we have \begin{align*} \delta V \frac{\partial c_B}{\partial t}&= -\delta\theta \delta z \delta r \frac{\partial  r J_{B,r}(r)}{\partial r} \\% &-\delta{}r \delta{}z \delta\theta \frac{\partial J_{B,\theta}(\theta)}{\partial \theta}\\% &- r \delta r \delta\theta \delta z \frac{\partial J_{B,z}(z)}{\partial z} \\% & +\delta V \sigma_B \end{align*}
  • Now we divide by the volume $\delta V = r \delta r \delta\theta \delta z$ to yield…
\begin{align*} \frac{\partial c_B}{\partial t}&= -\frac{1}{r} \frac{\partial  r J_{B,r}(r)}{\partial r}-\frac{1}{r} \frac{\partial J_{B,\theta}(\theta)}{\partial \theta} - \frac{\partial J_{B,z}(z)}{\partial z} +\sigma_B \end{align*}
  • Voila! The general balance equation in cylindrical coordinates.
  • Generating this balance equation is quite difficult; however, we don't have to repeat this to generate the momentum and mass balance.
  • What we would like to have, is have the cylindrical and rectangular (and spherical) balance equations in a more general form which doesn't take as much work to obtain each time.
  • In an ideal world we would like them to be the same! \begin{align*} \frac{\partial c_B}{\partial t} = -\nabla\cdot \boldsymbol{J}_B + \sigma_B \end{align*}
\begin{align*} \frac{\partial c_B}{\partial t}&= -\frac{1}{r} \frac{\partial  r J_{B,r}(r)}{\partial r}-\frac{1}{r} \frac{\partial J_{B,\theta}(\theta)}{\partial \theta} - \frac{\partial J_{B,z}(z)}{\partial z} +\sigma_B \end{align*}
  • If we look at the flux in cylindrical coordinates, we can be consider it to be a vector of the form \begin{align*} \boldsymbol{J}_B=\begin{bmatrix} J_{B,r},& J_{B,\theta},& J_{B,z} \end{bmatrix} \end{align*}
  • If we then say the equation above is the general balance equation, but in cylindrical coordinates… \begin{align*} \frac{\partial c_B}{\partial t} = -\nabla\cdot \boldsymbol{J}_B + \sigma_B \end{align*}
  • We end up with a definition of the gradient/del operator dotted with a vector in cylindrical coordinates ( Note: we cannot define a general $\nabla$ vector from this comparison): \begin{align*} \nabla\cdot \boldsymbol{v} = \frac{1}{r} \frac{\partial }{\partial r} r v_r + \frac{1}{r} \frac{\partial }{\partial \theta} v_\theta + \frac{\partial}{\partial z}v_z \end{align*}
\begin{align*} \frac{\partial c_B}{\partial t} = -\nabla\cdot \boldsymbol{J}_B + \sigma_B \end{align*}
  • This is a fundamental goal of vector notation, it is to become independent of the coordinate system !
  • This means we do not have to re-derive the continuity equation or the momentum balance equation, we only need to take the general equations \begin{align*} \frac{\partial \rho}{\partial t} &= -\nabla\cdot \rho \boldsymbol{v}\\ \frac{\partial \boldsymbol{v}}{\partial t} &= -\boldsymbol{v}\cdot\nabla \boldsymbol{v} - \rho^{-1}\nabla\cdot\boldsymbol{\tau} - \rho^{-1}\nabla p + \boldsymbol{g} \end{align*}
  • And use the cylindrical definition of each of these scalar/vector/tensor operations 1 See Chapter A.6 of Bird, R. : \begin{align*} \boldsymbol{v} &= \begin{bmatrix} v_r, & v_\theta, & v_z \end{bmatrix} & \nabla s &= \begin{bmatrix} \frac{\partial  s}{\partial r}, & \frac{1}{r} \frac{\partial  s}{\partial \theta}, & \frac{\partial  s}{\partial z} \end{bmatrix} \\ \nabla \cdot \boldsymbol{v} &= \frac{1}{r}\frac{\partial  r v_r}{\partial r} + \frac{1}{r} \frac{\partial  v_\theta}{\partial \theta} + \frac{\partial  v_z}{\partial z} & …&= … \end{align*}

  • A quick note about spherical coordinates.
  • In a spherical geometry our coordinates are specified by a radius $r$ , and two angles $\theta$ , $\phi$ .
  • You can derive the balance equation in exactly the same way as for the cylindrical coordinates.
  • We won't do this here as it is slightly more complicated than the cylinder, we'll just take the result.
  • Our definitions of a vector and the gradient operator in spherical coordinates are \begin{align*} \boldsymbol{v}&= \begin{bmatrix} v_r, & v_\theta, & v_\phi \end{bmatrix} \\ \nabla s &= \begin{bmatrix} \frac{\partial s}{\partial r}, & \frac{1}{r} \frac{\partial s}{\partial \theta} & \frac{1}{r\sin\theta}\frac{\partial s}{\partial \phi} \end{bmatrix} \\ \nabla\cdot \boldsymbol{v} &= \frac{1}{r^2}\frac{\partial  r^2 v_r}{\partial r} + \frac{1}{r\sin\theta} \frac{\partial  v_\theta \sin\theta}{\partial \theta} + \frac{1}{r\sin\theta}\frac{\partial v_\phi}{\partial \phi} \\ … &=… \end{align*}
    For more information on vector mechanics, coordinates take a look at Appendix A in BSL [1] 1 , the primary textbook for this course. [1] 1 Bird, R.
  • The vector continuity equation and momentum balance equation are the same in all coordinate systems \begin{align*} \frac{\partial \rho}{\partial t} &=- \nabla\cdot \rho \boldsymbol{v}\\ \frac{\partial \boldsymbol{v}}{\partial t} &= -\boldsymbol{v}\cdot\nabla \boldsymbol{v} - \rho^{-1}\nabla\cdot\boldsymbol{\tau} - \rho^{-1}\nabla p + \boldsymbol{g} \end{align*}
  • The definition of a vector and the gradient operator changes depending on the coordinate system
    • For rectangular/Cartesian coordinates we have two definitions, from which all operations are easily derived: \begin{align*} \boldsymbol{v} &= \begin{bmatrix} v_x, & v_y, & v_z \end{bmatrix}& \nabla &= \begin{bmatrix} \frac{\partial }{\partial x}, & \frac{\partial }{\partial y}, & \frac{\partial }{\partial z} \end{bmatrix} \end{align*}
    • For curvilinear coordinates, its more complicated, each operation involving one or more gradient operators $\nabla$ has a different definition due to the dependence of the directions of the coordinate system on the position.
    • For these terms, we use look up tables of expressions.
